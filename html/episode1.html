Daniela: Have you ever heard something and thought: “Damn.” It’s the kind of song, or passage, or, in my case, a poem that feels almost gut wrenching because of how true it feels. And it’s what I felt when I first heard a poem by Aoki Pink, –a poet I’ve gotten to know over several months of calls made from my dorm room at Barnard College in New York City to a homeless shelter in downtown Los Angeles, where Aoki currently lives. I study literature here at Barnard, but I am, admittedly, not usually drawn to poetry. Poems often feel abstract, like puzzles with no reference picture to go off from. Yet, Aoki’s poetry feels different. It’s rooted in realness. In life, love, struggle, and our nation’s most pertinent injustices. Take a Listen.

Daniela: Aoki was born in South Central L.A. on March 22, 1984. He was raised by his single mom, a lab technician at Cedar Sinai Hospital, and an older sister. He loved school, basketball, and frequenting the local record store to check out up-and-coming rappers and r&b music. As a kid, Aoki tested into a magnet school, got good grades, and had dreams of going to college on a basketball scholarship—life seemed promising for Aoki.

Aoki: My mom in the household she had us read and she was cultured, and she taught us about, you know, there's a life outside of this environment, don't be a product of your environment. Do more, seek more, learn more, want more. But it's like, sometimes your environment will engulf you.

Daniela: Then, when he started coming to terms with his gender and his sexuality, everything changed.

Aoki: I was born a girl and everything, but I was always just, I guess mischievious or rambunctious because my sister was always trying to stop me from climbing on stuff jumping up and down, getting dirty, keep your hands out of your pocket always had this little thing going on about myself, I've always been a tomboy. Aoki: I've always felt out of place. Like there was something wrong with me. Aoki: I was raised in a Christian home, and because of my sexuality, I was pushed out of my household. And that began my path, you know, I guess to deviancy. Or just, messing up.

Daniela: Being raised in a traditional Christian household, coming out wasn’t an option for Aoki. Experiencing gender dysphoria was something he was taught, from an early age, to suppress and correct.

After then, Aoki’s mother only became more wary of his masculinity– a wariness which finally erupted that regular weekday morning Aoki refused to wear a dress to school.

Aoki: And I tried to go live with my dad and he wasn't tripping on me that I wanted to wear overalls, jeans, and tennis shoes. Aoki: I wanted my mom to be happy with me. I wanted my mom to be pleased with me, I wanted my mom to love me. I didn't want my mom to be mad at me. I didn't want to get a whopping. You know, I wanted us to have fun, you know, and her to love me.

Daniela: According to a 2017 (twenty seventeen) study conducted by the University of Chicago, Black or African American youth are 83% (eighty-three percent) more likely to experience homelessness than other ethnic groups. And LGBTQ+ youth? They’re at risk of experiencing homelessness at a rate 120% (one hundred twenty percent) higher than non-LGBTQ+ youth. Being rejected by caretakers or loved ones is the study’s most cited reason for why LGBTQ+ youth experience homelessness (True Colors United Organization).

Daniela: Aoki ended up dropping out of Hamilton Magnet school in the 11th grade. At this point, he had transitioned to a group home in Hollywood, and started attending Hollywood Adult School. During his time there, he met a girl and started hanging around Hollywood boulevard –where he would drink, smoke weed, and steal to get by.

Aoki: We would walk the boulevard and we had our little cuts that we would go smoke weed, you know, and rapping. I thought I was just the shit. I just wanted to dress how I wanted to dress and my mom was not having it.

Daniela: No longer under the roof of a traditional Christian household, Aoki was finally able to present himself as he had always wanted to. Still, living on the streets came with its own challenges.

Forced to live in public spaces, people experiencing homelessness are at higher risk of interacting with the criminal justice system. The relationship between prisons and homeless shelters is often referred to as a “revolving door” as, not only are formerly incarcerated people 10 times more likely to be homeless than the general population, but homeless people are also more likely to be targeted by the police (Prison Policy Initiative).

“Public nuisance” crimes, including camping, loitering, and public urination, directly criminalize homelessness, and make it difficult for those facing homelessness to avoid the prison system. According to the National HCH (or Health Care for the Homeless) Council, having a history of homelessness is 7.5 to 11.3 times more likely among the prison population than the general population (National HCH Council).

Aoki: My big brother Will, and Dayaye, and Will's baby mom, Melody, they took me in and made sure that I have a roof over my head, like I didn't have to sleep on the street too many nights and I asked them if I needed to shower, take a shower and get dressed and I was like pillar to post. And then I got a job under the table at security. You know? I was living in my neighborhood. I grew up off of West Boulevard. You know, off of Crenshaw Hyde Park and I used to hang out on West Boulevard. That was my everyday, you know, stomping grounds. I used to visit the liquor store every day at 6am, every day that God sent, till the day I was like 18, 19.

Aoki: So that was my routine, I would get up and I would hang out and we would get faded, like, we would get dust.

Aoki: And we would rap because the only thing I had left was words. And poetry was free

Daniela: Homeless youth, in particular, are at high risk of partaking in risky behavior on the streets, with 20-30% of unstably housed young people having arrest histories and about 150,000 entering the criminal justice system annually (National HCH Council). Yet, since formerly incarcerated people face discrimination when seeking employment and housing, it makes it even harder for them to re-enter society.

Ever since being kicked out of his family home as a teenager, Aoki too has been caught up in this revolving door.

Aoki: So right now I'm in the shelter. I'm in the Weingart Center for the homeless, which is downtown LA. So it's like re–entry housing, kind of, kind of like a half-way program within another program for the homeless. I'm really grateful for it because, you know, otherwise, I’d just be outside on the streets.

Aoki: I was reluctant to live in this area, because it's so hard, like, this is like ground zero, you know what I mean? Either it’s going to make you or is going to take you or break you. Like those three things.

Daniela: The area Aoki is referring to is Skid Row, which is known to serve as a place of respite for both impoverished artists and POC. Nicknamed the “homeless capital of the United States,” researchers estimate that “28 to 60 percent” of the Skid Row population “suffer from mental [health issues]” and “30 to 80 percent have drug and alcohol addictions” (Stuart).

Here’s an archival clip from a 2018 Al Jazeera news story which sent a journalist to Skid Row shortly after the neighborhood became the focus of a report written by the United Nations on extreme poverty:

Reporter: It’s really hard to describe how difficult the circumstances are for living out here in Skid Row. There are so many people who are battling mental illness. A lot of people got triggered just by seeing us with our cameras.

Resident: You’re gonna get your a** killed on Skid Row about that camera.

Reporter: We’ve seen syringes in the street. We’ve seen feces in the street. You can smell the lack of sanitation. So, not only is this a major homeless crisis, it’s a public health crisis and a mental health crisis too.”

Daniela: Apart from the neighborhood’s troubling living conditions, there’s its unusually high crime rate. Skid Row is policed about 69 times more than the rest of Los Angeles (Stuart). In 2006 and 2007 alone, 18,000 arrests were made in the neighborhood, though it only had 15,000 residents at the time. Many more citations were issued for littering, jay-walking, and sitting on the sidewalk—just the kind of policies which disproportionately affect homeless residents (Stuart). Aoki, who has been in and out of Skid Row since he was 18 years old, hopes one day he might save enough money to make it out of the neighborhood and live on his own.

Aoki: I just want to move to an area where I'm seeing something different that I've never seen. I can't take credit for this, you know, Lionel Richie has a song called “Zoom.” That song, he has a line in it, and he goes about horizons that he may never see, you know what I mean? It's like I'm trying to get to some of those horizons and see them.

Daniela: What I’ve realized by talking to Aoki is that sometimes he speaks in song lyrics, connecting to the words profoundly, finding analogies in them to his own life. Sometimes he’ll send me song recommendations after we chat. LL Cool J, Common, Ludacris —artists I, admittedly, know by name, but not by their discography. The reason he’s an R&B connoisseur, I figure, is because he’s good at recognizing when words tap at something true.
